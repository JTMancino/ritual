Rules of the Game

Below are the rules of how to play Ritual TTRPG. Remember, as a role playing game, nothing is more important than your imagination and no personal change or addition to these rules is off-limits at the table if it will make your party's experience more enjoyable.

The Auran

1.1 Aurans are the gods of the world of Ritual, and masters of mortal fate. Similar to the dungeon or game master of other games, one player of Ritual will always be set aside to manage the world and non-player characters (NPCs) that the other players, called the Fatebound, interact with. In Ritual, this person is called The Auran. For all matters of story, mechanics, judgment, and direction, The Auran's word is final. They are not intended to be an enemy to the Fatebound, but a key facilitator in allowing the story of Ritual to come to life in an organic and fun way while minimizing disagreements and confusion at the play table.

1.2 Aurans decide the details of towns, wilderness, ancient ruins, and everything in between. They can use monsters straight from the bestiary to attack the Fatebound, modify them, or invent their own. They can make NPCs that assist players, pose as obstacles, or offer quests. The purpose of this freedom is to stop Ritual from becoming, contrary to the name, overly ritualistic. The game should remain fresh for both the Auran and the Fatebound as they explore together.

Building a Character

2.1 Unlike The Auran, who builds and maintains a Ritual world, players interact with it through the lens of a character they create. Characters are people with special powers compared to ordinary folk, but nonetheless fit naturally into the structure of Ritual and its environment. Players are intended to give their characters personality, quirks, and ambitions, then identify with them as they use them to explore the world of Ritual.

2.2 Players differentiate their characters from each other first by means of giving them a name, a backstory, and a personality. Not all people in the world of Ritual are the same however, and there will be mechanical differences from character to character. These differences are represented principally by attributes, classes and subclasses, and equipment. The detailed rules regarding these differences can be found later in the rules as well as explored in the character creation tool.

2.3 The following is a table of superclasses (arcanist, frontiersman, soldier) and their subclasses.

Arcanist Frontiersman Soldier
Artificer Deputy Crusader
Druid Outlaw Guerilla
Evoker Physician Grenadier
Mystic Ranger Witch Hunter

These classes each represent a path of expertise a given character may go down in the course of their adventuring lives. Arcanists are the rare few who can speak the language of the gods, Auran, and therefore possess magical power. Frontiersman are hardy and rugged adventurers, who know an oppurtunity when they see one and rarely flinch from danger. Soldiers are dominators on the battlefield, wielding heavy arms and armor to protect their allies and slay their enemies. It is reccomended to develop a character concept first, then ask yourself which superclass and subclass best fits the character you envisioned yourself playing.

2.4 At level one, players start with 1d4 + 3 hit points. A hit point is a measure of health, which will decrease when something takes damage as described in chapter 4.5. If most creatures or objects reach zero hitpoints, they die or are destroyed. Be careful! Fatebound may get far more powerful as their levels increase, but their mortality does not change. Each character has a set of attributes, and a pool of attribute points to spend at character creation. You receive 100 points to distribute as you please between brawn, charisma, dexterity, intellect, and willpower. These traits have no direct impact on routine aspects of the game, but their relevance arises either in connection to effects with reference them or during special events called Auran trials, as explained in rule 2.5. For example, an outlaw frontiersman who just killed an giant may go to pick up his hammer, only to find it requires a brawn of seventy five to properly wield, or an artificer arcanist may find themselves lucky enough to notice a hidden door that requires an intellect of thirty to perceive. Finally, there are focus points. Focus points are a resource which some classes need to manage, and each character has a base of three maximum points. Every character has a base of three maximum focus, which may be modified by some effects and abilities to a minimum of one.

2.5 Auran trials are challenges which require a throw of some number of dice decided by The Auran. The aim is for Fatebound to roll a number below their ability score, and there will be consequences for passing or failing the roll. For example, The Auran may tell each Fatebound to roll 3d6 against their brawn to swim to shore from a sinking ship. The crusader with a brawn of twenty will pass automatically, because 3d6 cannot total higher than his brawn score. On the other hand, the physician with a brawn of five will need to roll exceptionally low in order to pass. For most characters it will be impossible for an ability score to exceed 100, so in the special case of a 1d100 Auran trial, the player may consider their ability score equal to a percent chance of passing the trial. All base scores are increased by one for each level, so even with no investment in a given ability, an average level ten character will have a ten percent chance at passing a 1d100 Auran trial in that ability. Generally speaking, Auran trials will only be used to test for success under duress or for feats of great difficulty. Even a very weak Fatebound will have no trouble opening an unlocked door or noticing a shining golden throne.

Races, Levels and Classes

3.1 A character's level is an abstract way to represent their skill, strength, and experience gained by adventuring. Characters begin at level one unless directed otherwise by The Auran, and The Auran decides when characters can increase in level. It is generally reccomended that all players of a given party increase in level at the same time to ensure a balanced play experience. In addition to increasing progression of a class when leveling up, players may distribute an additional ten attribute points to a maximum of 100 in each attribute. At level one, Fatebound characters choose a class which they will stick with. Each class, arcanist, frontiersman, and soldier, offer unique benefits and grow more powerful as levels increase. At subsequent levels, players can progress in the same or a different class. Here is a table of those superclass benefits by level.

Arcanist Frontiersman Soldier
You can communicate in Auran, the language of the gods. You can use an action to read Auran scrolls to cast a spell inscribed upon one. This will destroy the scroll, but in exchange trigger a magnificent effect. In addition, you may use an action and expend one focus point to directly deal 1d4 magic damage to a target creature in sight with a destructive phrase. You are proficient in the use of longbows, crossbows, and light armor. After years of honing your craft's precision, you know just how to land a hit. When your unmodified attack roll is 90 or higher, your attack lands in a critical spot and deals double damage to its target. You are a battle hardened warrior whose presence dominates the front line of combat. You are proficient in all light, medium, and heavy armor, as well as melee weapons and shields. In addition, neither the armor you wear nor the weapons you wield encumber you as you have mastered their use and they have become an extension of yourself.
Your maximum focus increases by two, as you learn to think fluently in the Auran tongue. In addition, you gain access to arcanist subclasses, and may progress one whenever you take a level in arcanist. Your danger instincts are honed to a razor edge, and you can add fifteen to each initiative roll you make. In addition, you gain access to frontiersman subclasses, and may progress one whenever you take a level in frontiersman. Dedicated practice has made your combat form impeccable. When you attack with a weapon, ignore five of the enemy's armor and increase the damage you deal by one. In addition, you gain access to soldier subclasses, and may progress one whenever you take a level in soldier.
The wind whispers Auran into your ears when magic is nearby; you can sense active sources of magic within ten feet of you. You gain knowledge of where it is and what school it belongs to. You know how to track your quarry. When you make a ranged attack, you may ignore ten of the enemy's armor. When you make a melee attack, your level one ability's critical attack range triggers on a roll of 85 and higher. You learn to carefully control the path of your strikes. When you miss an enemy with a weapon attack, you may choose to do one damage anyway.
You learn to wield dangerous but powerful blood magic. When you run out of focus, you may use your hit points in its place expending them as if they were focus. Your reflexes have been honed to a razor's edge in order to keep you alive. When you are hit by an attack, you may expend one focus to force the attacker to reroll. You gain proficiency with crossbows. You may attack with ranged weapons at melee range without penalty, as if they were a melee weapon.
You have mastered the art of drawing on the power of the gods. If you go at least one full hour without expending any focus, you may recover all of your focus. Your perceptive nature and survival instinct are unmatched. Once per turn you may take a free dodge action. You are an avatar of death on the battlefield. Once per turn when you hit with a weapon attack, you may expend one focus to add 1d4 damage to that attack.

Arcanist Subclasses

3.2 Artificer

The artificer arcanist is a master of enchanted items and treasures. They are able to identify, assess, and improve items found by looting or purchased at stores as well as help to avoid or set traps and utilize a wide range of magic stored in Auran scrolls.

Artificer
The artificer is a master of the Auran tongue's written form. They can copy Auran scrolls found on their adventures to make use of the spells more than once, so long as they have an hour per scroll to spare and a collection of materials which The Auran deems of high enough quality and expense. They are proficient with light armor and melee weapons.
Artificers can improve basic items they find on their adventures. By expending a focus point, they can cause a single weapon to deal magic damage and ignore 5 armor per artificer level. If you enchant a new weapon in this way, the magic will fade from the old one.
Not only can artificers add enchantments, they can also remove them. With an hour and a focus point, artificers can render magical weapons and armor inert, which is useful to remove curses or prepare a blank slate for better enchantments. Some magical items may be protected against such tinkering.
Master artificers can perform far more than basic enchantments. If an artificer of this level dedicates a full 24 hours of study to an enchanted item, they may learn to replicate its enchantment in a similar way to their basic improvements by expending three focus points instead of one.

3.3 Druid

The druid arcanist is a nature inspired warrior. Leaning into the roots of Auran as the language of creation, they share in the glory of the world the gods wrought.

Druid
The druid is not bound by the flesh they were born with. When you make an unarmed attack, you may expend one focus to make the attack instead do 3d4 magic damage.
Druids can restore damage to their bodies by chanting the old Auran hymns. You may use an action to expend one focus point and restore 1d4 hit points to yourself.
By spending a minute in deep trance and expending a focus point, a druid may transform themselves into an insect, rodent, bird, or fish for five minutes.
Druids can cause uncontrollable plant growth in a five foot area using an action and a focus point. This may provide food, thorns, or any number of creative advantages at The Auran's discretion.

3.4 Evoker

The evoker arcanist is a powerhouse of raw magical energy. They direct bursts of magic power to damage their enemies from afar.

Evoker
Evokers are proficient in longbows and crossbows. When an evoker makes a weapon attack, they may expend one focus point to instead make the damage magical and add one to it.
Evokers do not need to rely on physical weaponry. If they are not wielding a weapon, they may use an action to summon a spectral version of a weapon they can use which lasts until the end of their turn. Attacks with this weapon ignore five armor, do magical damage, and add one to all damage rolls.
In time, evokers learn to passively infuse their attacks with Auran energy. All attacks you make may instead do magical damage.
An evoker can redirect magical energy, protecting themselves instead of causing harm. In response to taking damage, an evoker may instead reduce that damage by expending an equivalent number of focus points.

3.5 Mystic

The mystic arcanist is a sholarly and enigmatic person. They use Auran to communicate with a world beyond mortal perception, often seeming to talk to themselves when the truth is far more frightening.

Mystic
The mystic can command unseen spirits to move objects, expending a focus point and using an action to shift any object they can see up to ten feet in any direction.
Mystics can sometimes call directly on Auran gods, they have a 20% chance of not destroying a scroll when they read it.
Mystics can make deals with less than reputable spirits, using a focus point and a hit point to reroll random results which they find unfavorable.
Very powerful mystics can study a scroll for 24 hours to memorize it. When they do so, they may expend three focus points and an action to cast the inscribed spell as if they had a scroll for it.

Frontiersman Subclasses

3.6 Deputy

The deputy is a grizzled lawman. Ranging from town sheriff to private investigator, the skillset of a deputy involves an eye for trouble and a quick draw.

Deputy
You are proficient in melee weapons, firearms, and medium armor. Your duties as a lawman, or something similar, taught you the value of all of them.
You are an expert horse rider, and a trusty steed always seems to find you when you're in need. It acts on your initiative and obeys your mental commands, though it does not attack in combat. The horse is so uncannily adept at finding and understanding you, it might even be a spirit.
The deputy is an expert at cutting off enemy retreat and claiming their bounties. When you hit with a weapon attack, the target loses five speed on their next turn and their armor is reduced by five for one hour.
You may expend a focus point and use an action to mark a bounty. If the target of your bounty dies within the next five seconds, you regain the focus point and whoever killed it (including you if applicable) regains an additional 1d4 as well.

3.7 Outlaw

The outlaw is a criminal foil to the deputy. They have lived a rough life on the fringes of civilization, and for one reason or another, know how to avoid capture very well.

Outlaw
You are proficient in melee weapons, firearms, and lock picks. Your time on the run, or something similar, taught you the value of all of them.
If you fail to conceal an action you tried to keep hidden such as sneaking, stealing, or picking a lock, you may expend a focus point to try again.
You are a master of disguise and with an hour can completely change your appearance. You can use an action to trade places with a willing ally within ten feet.
You may expend a focus point to mark a bounty. If the target of your bounty dies within the next five seconds, you find 1d10 extra gold on the body regardless of the creature type.

3.8 Physician

Physicians have seen the worst of what the world can do to a mortal body. Whether a traveling plague doctor or a wilderness apothecary, these hardened medics should not be underestimated in combat. If they can fix a body with precision, they can break one too.

Physician
You are proficient in melee weapons. You can expend an action and a focus point to tend a creature in melee range, healing one hit point.
With an hour and the right ingredients, you can create a dose of poison or medicine with effects described by The Auran. Your critical hit range increases by five.
You are a master of battlefield medicine. When you tend a creature, restore 1d4 hitpoints instead of one. Alternatively, you may expend two focus to heal a scar.
As a master anatomist, you know where all the vital spots are. When you score a critical hit, instead triple its damage.

3.9 Ranger

Rangers are true paragons of wilderness exploration and survival They are quintessential scouts and guides.

Ranger
You are proficient in one additional weapon of your choice. Once every turn, you may move up to ten feet without taking an action. In addition, impeding terrain no longer slows your movement, as you are too accustomed to traversing the wilds.
You always know the direction of North, and cannot get lost except by magical means. Enemies you hit with weapon attacks lose 5 armor for one hour as you degrade their defenses.
You can see the signs left by passing creatures, and can choose to destroy them when you do. When you miss an enemy with a ranged weapon attack, you may expend one focus to hit instead.
You can attempt to tame one creature at a time as a traveling and combat companion. It acts on your initiative and obeys your mental commands. If the creature is more powerful than a wolf or horse, it will require an Auran trial.

Soldier Subclasses

3.10 Crusader

Crusaders are passionate and stubborn warriors who fight for a cause. It will take much more than normal to kill one of these.

Crusader
Your maximum hitpoints increase by two. If your maximum hitpoints are lower than seven after this, they increase by one more.
When an effect says it restores hitpoints, and you are at full hitpoints, you may instead permanently remove one scar.
You can spend a focus point to mark an enemy you see as your rival. You restore one hitpoint when that rival dies, but instead lose one hitpoint if they remain alive after 24 hours.
You can attempt to martyr yourself for your cause. In combat, you may willingly take 1d4 damage to deal an extra 3d4 damage when you attack.

3.11 Guerilla

Guerillas are stealthy and cunning freedom fighters. They are versatile warriors with a range of options available.

Guerilla
You are proficient in stringed ranged weapons, and may expend a focus point and use an action to detonate a smoke bomb, filling an area ten feet around you with harmless obscuring fog for five seconds.
When you attack an enemy that cannot see you, you may add one damage to the attack. If the enemy did not know you were there, you instead add 1d4.
When you hit an enemy with a ranged weapon, you may move up to ten feet for free.
You can sense your environment in ten feet in any direction as if you could see it, even if blinded or in the dark. You can see most hidden and invisible creatures and objects in this way.

3.12 Grenadier

Grenadiers are powerful ranged fighters which rely on explosives and chemical weaponry to devastating effect.

Grenadier
You are proficient in firearms and can throw handeld projectiles such as grenades and potion bottles twice as far.
When you attack an enemy with a handheld projectile, you cannot miss by more than five feet as long as they are within range.
When you hit an enemy with a firearm, once per turn you may take the reload action for free.
You are adept at scavenging for explosives. You know what actions are likely to cause environmental effects, and can generally find random materials enough to produce one free grenade per day in almost any environment.

3.13 Witch Hunter

Witch hunters are the bane of paranormal monsters and evil spellcasters. They dedicate themselves to destroying that which violates the natural order.

Witch Hunter
Your weapon attacks all count as silvered, and you know how to apply silver to weapons when you have the supply. In addition, your melee weapon attacks all count as doing magic damage against incorporeal creatures.
You fear no evil. When you take magic damage, treat it as if it were regular damage instead and reduce it by one.
The soul of a witch hunter must be a fortress. You are immune to mind altering magical effects.
You can expend a focus point and an action to suppress all magic within five feet of you for five seconds.

Races

3.14 Each character has a race, which adds a small bonus to their chosen playstyle. There are five principle races, as described in the lore page, and their descriptions and advantages are summarized here.

Firstborn Earthspawn Skyspawn Seaspawn Flamespawn
Humans, or by their proper title Firstborn, are the first creations of the Aurans and are made in their image. They are the most populous and most colonial of all races, and their presence sprawls across the entire world of ritual. They form massive towns and cities which are sometimes sustained by as many as a dozen rituals of all different types and cultures. Earthspawn are a humanoid people who live together in tightly knit mountain fortress communities, which can be distrustful of outsiders. Their skin is made of gleaming bronze, and when there is a light source nearby they emit a soft reflective glow. They often adorn themselves with jewels and rare metals from the Earth where they live, and once they initiate a ritual they take pride in continuously sustaining it for generations. Seaspawn are long lived amphibious humanoids who populate the oceans, though they can survive just as easily on land and a rare few do venture up. Their culture has been allowed to develop safe from other races, and without war or old age, have developed a thriving culture of arts and sciences. Most population centers under the sea employ very weak rituals, if any, because the water naturally protects them from danger Skyspawn are nomadic winged humanoids with the appearance of angels who move together in nomadic tribes. Skyspawn tribes must recreate their rituals each time they move, so many tribes have a very high number of arcanists that can help rebuild it daily. Some tribes actually speak Auran as a primary language. Flamespawn are animated humanoid golems made from still glowing embers and coal. They do not reproduce in the traditional sense, but they spring into existence out from the ash at the center of massive wildfires. Some fire siblings, who emerge from the heart of the same flame, build primitive shelters and live together where they were born. Their rituals involve lighting renewable fires, scattering the ashes to fertilize new life. Others travel to nearby towns and cities, or strike out entirely on their own, guided by instinct and the lessons imparted by whatever spirit possessed them.
Humans can allocate ten additional ability points at character creation to represent their diversity and versatility. The maximum for any individual ability is still 51. Your danger instincts are honed to a razor edge, and you can add ten to each initiative roll you make. In addition, you gain access to the first level of a frontiersman subclass of your choice. The Earthspawn metallic skin naturally adds five armor at all times. Because of their vibrant culture, a Seaspawn's maximum focus is increased by one and they can move freely and breathe underwater. Flamespawn emit a dim light out to five feet, and are immune to damage from excessive heat.

Actions and Combat

4.1 Your character's decisions in Ritual are incredibly freeform for most of the game, but combat offers more structure. Time is divided into turns, where creatures alternate taking actions, of a five second span. Each creature can take up to three actions during their turn, and there are base actions shared by all creatures then special actions defined by classes, subclasses, items, or The Auran. See the common actions below.

Action Description
Attack Roll 1d100, and compare the number to the target's armor rating. If you rolled above the armor rating (or scored a critical hit as a frontiersman) you damage the target for an amount determined by the weapon. Unarmed strikes do one damage unless stated otherwise. Some weapons have ranges, and almost all will modify the attack action in some way. Ranged attacks against enemies in melee range treat that enemy as if they have twenty additional armor.
Move You walk, run, or sprint in any direction. Some terrain is impeding, and will therefore halve your movement speed, other effects or abilities may increase the speed as well, but the standard movement speed of a Fatebound is 25 feet per five seconds.
Dodge Pick a target or source of damage. For the next five seconds, your armor against it is increased by ten. If it is causing some sort of unavoidable damage instead of attacking, you may reduce that damage by one unless specified otherwise by The Auran.
Interact Pick up, move, change, or store an item within your reach.
Hide Make a dexterity Auran trial. If you pass, your armor is increased by ten against all targets who rely on sight to attack you. You are unseen by them.

4.2 Armor is a measure of how likely a creature is to be hit. The base armor of a Fatebound is thirty, meaning there is a 30% chance that they will be hit by a creature targeting them. This number can be increased by abilities and items, but can never exceed 90 for Fatebound or NPCs unless specifically overruled by The Auran.

Hit points are a measure of a creatures health. Damage reduces the current hit points from a pool up to the maximum, and when any damage occurs on a creature with zero hit points remaining a scar will form. For unimportant NPCs, a scar will typically mean instant death. For Fatebound and legendary NPCs however, up to three scars may be accrued before a hit becomes fatal. The fourth scar will kill you instantly. When you get a scar, roll 1d12. Consult the table below to see what kind will form. Hitpoints cannot go negative, and once your hitpoints are brought to zero you only need to track sources of damage, not quantity.

Result Scar
1 You lose a limb. Roll 1d4, or 1d6 if you are a Skyspawn. On a 1-2 you lose an arm, on a 3-4 you lose a leg, and on a 5-6 you lose a wing. Even when the scar is healed, your limb will not naturally regrow.
2 You break an arm. Choose your left or right arm. Items heavier than one pound can no longer be lifted by that arm, and you can not make attacks with it until this scar is healed.
3 You are struck in the head and fall unconscious. You can do nothing until you wake up. An ally can wake you with an action, but on your turn the only action you can take is to roll 1d10. On a 10, you rouse yourself.
4 You break a leg or, if you are a Skyspawn, roll 1d4. On a 1-2 you break a leg, on a 3-4 you break a wing. Choose an appropriate limb, if it is a leg you may not take the move action to walk or swim more than once per turn until this scar is healed. If it is a wing, you cannot fly until this scar is healed.
9 You are losing too much blood. Your maximum hitpoints are reduced by three until this scar is healed.
5 Your wound is infected, or dirtied. Until this scar is healed, all sources of healing are reduced by one to a minimum of one.
6 The stress of combat overtakes you. On your next turn, you may take only the move and prepare actions.
7 Blood streams down your face, obstructing your vision. You cannot see further than five feet by normal means for one minute.
10 You twist your ankle, causing you to stumble and drop what you were carrying in your hands.
8 Your body is brutally damaged. This wound will leave a grisly visual scar, even long after it is healed.
11 The fear of combat overtakes you, and you briefly lose control. Roll 1d4. On a 1-2 you urinate in your armor. On a 2-4 you likewise defecate. Flamespawn are immune to this effect, though they still are scarred.
12 Your life flashes before your eyes and you realize there is still much to live for. Though you are still brought closer to death by this mental scar, heal 1d4 hitpoints and rejoin the fight if you are still alive.

4.3 To determine the turn order in combat, The Auran will call for an iniative roll. Each creature in the vicinity will roll 1d100, and combat will progress from the creature with the highest to the lowest roll. Some abilities or items may augment your natural roll. Initiative can neither exceed 100 nor fall below one, and ties will be broken randomly.

4.4 In combat you will expend a resource called focus. Focus regenerates at a rate of one focus per hour without expending any. It remains until it is expended, up to the character's maximum as determined by the character creation rules in chapter 2.4.

4.5 Damage can come from many sources. Sometimes a number will be specified, like a dagger causing 1d4 damage or a monster's claw dealing 2d6. Other times, The Auran will decide arbitrarily, like in the case of a player eating rotten fruit off a long abandoned plate. The Auran may decide circumstances merit an increase or decrease to damage, like if a player stuffs a lit torch into the mouth of a hostile elemental, an ice elemental will likely take more damage the case of a fire elemental. Damage reduction, like the kind associated with heavy and medium armor, means that every source of damage is reduced by that number. For example, a musket which does 1d10+2 damage when fired into a character in heavy armor will actually only do 1d10, because the +2 cancels out with the 2 damage reduction heavy armor affords. When damage is described as magic or magical, it means there is some special degree of Auran energy infusing it. This may cause arbitrary effects, but some are also predictable. Magic attacks work like normal against incorporeal creatures, and ignore the damage reduction trait of armor.

4.6 Flying creatures may move in any direction, not just along the ground. Winged creatures require speed to stay airborn, and will safely glide to the ground at the end of their turn if they do not use all of their actions to move. Creatures which fly by other means may or may not be subject to this restriction.

4.7 Should a creature fall uncontrollably from a great height, for example if a Skyspawn breaks their wing midair, they will take damage from falling. The amount of damage is equal to one for each five feet fallen minus one, for example no damage will be taken from falling five feet, and four damage will be taken from falling twenty-five feet.

Economy and Treasure

5.1 On your adventures, you may find many items including but not limited to those listed in the shop. When adventuring, thieving, or looting a battlefield, The Auran decides what valuable objects may remain to be salvaged. Finding items in this way, buying them, or crafting them, are all ways to add items to your inventory. The items in your character's inventory are carried by you, and may be used creatively to solve problems or affect change.

5.2 Equipment and treasure you find may encumber you. If any character carries more than 30 kilograms (kg) of items and gear, their movement speed will be halved from all nonmagical sources and they cannot fly with their wings. A kilogram is equal to about 2.2 pounds. If a character carries more than 50 kg, they must use two actions rather than one to move. Under normal circumstances, a character cannot carry more than 70 kg.

Items in Cradle are bought and sold with gold currency. The basic unit of currency is a gram of gold, referred to as a shaving. For comparison, this is about the weight of a paperclip. Some places may mint gold into pre-weighed coins for trade, some may mold it into rough pieces of equal size, and some may literally shave it and approximate the weight continuously. For very large purchases, gold may be measured in bars, which are equal to one kilogram or 1000 shavings. Because encumbrance is calculated in the imperial system for a predominantly U.S. American audience, the weight of gold shavings should be generally ignored and the weight of gold bars can be treated as two pounds.
